An essay profile · ENGL 1101 · Joshua Kalamba

Why do they write?

A profile of Mr. R — retirement home manager, husband, and writer of weekly love letters — and the investigation into why.

Georgia State University Prof. Williams March 2026
The subject
On the surface
Lanky. Dark-haired. A face of near-permanent neutrality. He moved through the retirement home with authoritative calm — sharp instructions, no idle conversation.
The crack in the mask
When Mrs. R appeared, everything shifted. His face lightened. His shoulders dropped. He'd touch her arm in passing, lean close to make her smile. Seconds. Then gone.
The question it raised
Who is this man outside work? That stark gap between the professional and the tender raised one question: does writing have any part in it?

The investigation
Step 1 — observation

Looking for writing at work

As a server at the retirement home, the only writing visible was functional: invoices, paychecks, quick notes to staff. Nothing of substance. His reason for writing was not clear — only that the reason wasn't professional.

Finding: look elsewhere
Step 2 — web search

Searching for him online

A robotic staff biography on the retirement home website — almost certainly not written by him. No social media. No LinkedIn. No published writing of any kind. The absence was itself information: whatever he wrote, it was private.

Finding: writing is outside work scope
Step 3 — the interview

A Saturday afternoon in the break room

He arrived on time. Sat across with one leg over the other, arms folded, expression unreadable. Then came the question about the origin of his writing — and he answered without hesitation. Everything opened up.

Finding: the answer is love
In his own words

"I asked him once what he was writing. He said: 'Everything I cannot say aloud.'"

Mr. R recalling his father — who composed letters to his mother at the kitchen table every night after dinner. The phrase became a philosophy before Mr. R ever chose it consciously.

"I noticed I was saying things in those letters I could not say in person. Things I meant deeply, but that felt too large for a conversation."

On the two years he spent apart from his future wife, writing to her weekly. He discovered that letters could carry weight that conversation couldn't hold.

"You cannot write as fast as you feel — so you have to choose what matters."

On the mechanics of writing: how the physical slowness of putting words down forces emotional precision. Feeling is fast. Writing is a filter.

"A letter stays. You can hold it. You can come back to it when you need to remember. A letter is a record that someone saw you."

On why he still writes to his wife every week — even though they live and work together in the same building. The letter isn't about logistics. It's about evidence of being known.
The answer
Why does Mr. R write?
Love.

Not productivity. Not habit. Not professional obligation. Mr. R writes because it is the most precise and lasting form of love he knows how to give. Writing forces the slowness that feeling never allows — and produces something the spoken word cannot: an artifact you can hold, return to, and be seen by.

Why writing? Three reasons that converged
I
It began with inheritance

His father modeled it silently at the kitchen table. The phrase "everything I cannot say aloud" became a life philosophy before Mr. R ever consciously chose writing as his medium.

II
Distance proved it

Two years apart from the woman he would marry showed him that letters could carry what spoken conversation couldn't — depth, weight, permanence. The medium was tested under pressure and survived.

III
Proximity hasn't stopped it

He writes to his wife every week. They share a home, a building, a life. The letters continue because they were never about logistics. They are about being witnessed — intentionally, in writing, every week.

What writing does — according to Mr. R
Slows the mind

"You cannot write as fast as you feel" — writing introduces a productive lag between emotion and expression.

Forces selection

The slowness compels a question: what actually matters? What, from everything felt, is worth putting into permanent form?

Creates permanence

A letter stays. Unlike a conversation — which ends, fades, and is half-remembered — a letter can be held and returned to.

Bears witness

"A letter is a record that someone saw you." The deepest function of writing, for Mr. R, is not communication but recognition. It says: I looked at you carefully enough to put it into words.

Closing lines of the essay

"The interview provided useful insight into why Mr. R writes: love. With what was seen in the mere seconds when Mr. R interacted with his wife, and has finally been spoken aloud in the form of a story. The practice of writing was explained to be in the form of letters to his loved one which originated from a parent at the kitchen table. Mr. R explained that a letter is a record that someone saw you. It seems worth noting that this essay is one too."

— Joshua Kalamba, ENGL 1101, Georgia State University, March 2026
